Change 7 — Two-Stage Hurdle Model: Separating Occurrence from Magnitude

Generated 5 April 2026  |  Source: dowhy_mediation_analysis_90d_20260405_155838.csv  |  20 radii × 4 models  |  90-day lookback

1. Change Summary

Change 7 (ref. Feb 18 sponsor meeting) replaces the single-regression outcome model with a two-stage hurdle framework that explicitly separates the two physically distinct processes underlying induced seismicity. The prior pipeline modeled earthquake magnitude as a single continuous outcome using one regression equation, implicitly blending triggering probability and magnitude scaling into the same parameter estimate. This reduces interpretability and may bias the estimated injection effect because not all injection activity triggers events, and the factors that control whether an event occurs may differ from those that control how large it becomes.

The fix implements a hurdle model with two stages. Stage 1 fits a GBM binary classifier predicting whether an earthquake occurs at all (S > 0), using W, P, G1, G2, and P_missing as features. Stage 2 fits a GBM regressor predicting magnitude conditional on occurrence, trained only on rows where S > 0. The DAG structure and causal mediation analysis (Changes 1–6) are unchanged; the hurdle model runs in parallel as a separate characterization of the outcome distribution. All four model variants (OLS, GBM, RF, Lasso) share the same hurdle outputs per radius, as the hurdle is fit once per file independent of the causal estimator.

4. Key Findings

  • Stage 1 classifier performs very well across all radii. ROC-AUC ranges from 0.971 to 0.993 (mean 0.976), indicating the GBM classifier can almost perfectly discriminate between event and non-event rows using injection volume, pressure, and fault geometry alone. The highest AUC at 1 km (0.993) reflects cleaner event labeling near fault structures.
  • Occurrence probability declines with distance. P(occur) falls from 0.762 at 1 km to 0.646 at 20 km — a 15-percentage-point drop. This is consistent with the physical expectation that injection effects attenuate with distance from the wellbore, though as noted in Section 5, radial distance is an imperfect proxy for the true fault-plane propagation pathway.
  • Conditional magnitude is stable across distances. E[mag | occur] ranges narrowly from 1.634 to 1.747 across 20 radii (a span of 0.11 magnitude units). Once an event is triggered, the expected magnitude shows only weak distance dependence. This suggests the factors controlling whether an event occurs differ substantively from those controlling how large it becomes — exactly the motivation for the two-stage framework.
  • Stage 2 MAE is consistent and interpretable. Mean absolute error on conditional magnitude averages 0.436 magnitude units across all radii (range 0.418–0.457), with no systematic trend by distance. This is directly interpretable: the model's conditional magnitude predictions are within roughly 0.44 magnitude units on average.
  • The two-stage decomposition adds interpretability beyond the single-regression model. The prior pipeline's single regression conflated two effects into one coefficient. The hurdle framework now separates: (1) injection's role in triggering events (captured in Stage 1 with AUC > 0.97), and (2) injection's role in modulating magnitude given triggering (captured in Stage 2 with MAE ~ 0.44). These can now be reported and discussed independently.

5. Known Limitation — Formation Depth

Per asset geologist feedback (C. Reudelhueber, April 2026): shallow injection zones (Grayburg, San Andres, Glorieta, Clearfork) are physically disconnected from the deep-seated faults attributed to seismicity in the Permian Basin. Only deep injection zones (Devonian and below, principally Ellenburger) are connected to seismogenic faults. The current dataset does not differentiate by disposal formation — this information requires external well-completion records not available in the RRC injection data. Stage 1 partially learns this distinction through correlation with G1 and G2 (fault proximity geometry), but cannot cleanly separate shallow from deep disposal wells. The high Stage 1 AUC likely reflects strong spatial signal rather than complete formation-depth discrimination. Formation stratification remains the primary recommended extension once disposal-interval data become available.
