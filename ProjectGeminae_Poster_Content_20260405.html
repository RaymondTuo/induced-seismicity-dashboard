Project Geminae

Causal Estimation of Injection-Induced Seismicity in the Permian Basin

MS in Business Analytics Capstone  |  Tepper School of Business, Carnegie Mellon University  |  April 2026

ABSTRACT

Induced seismicity from saltwater disposal (SWD) injection in the Permian Basin has increased substantially over the past decade, raising operational and regulatory risk for operators. This project, conducted in partnership with an asset geologist at a Permian Basin operator, builds a fully causal pipeline to estimate the effect of injection activity on local earthquake magnitude across 1–20 km radii.

We implement a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG)-based mediation framework using the DoWhy library, with injection volume (W) as treatment, wellhead pressure (P) as mediator, nearest fault distance and fault segment count as confounders, and local earthquake magnitude as outcome. The pipeline applies nonlinear estimation via Gradient Boosted Machines and includes a two-stage hurdle model separating earthquake occurrence from conditional magnitude.

Across 344,000 well-event linkages at a 90-day lookback, the total causal effect of injection on magnitude is consistent and statistically significant at all 20 radii. The hurdle model finds a Stage 1 ROC-AUC of 0.97–0.99, with occurrence probability declining from 76% near-field to 65% far-field. Conditional magnitude given occurrence averages M 1.68 and is stable across distance.

INTRODUCTION

The Permian Basin (West Texas) accounts for a disproportionate share of U.S. induced seismicity. Operators injecting produced water into disposal wells alter subsurface pore pressure, which can reactivate pre-existing faults. Regulatory bodies and operators lack quantitative, causal tools to estimate injection-to-seismicity risk at the well level.

Existing approaches are primarily correlational, relying on OLS regression or proximity thresholds. These methods conflate triggering probability with magnitude scaling, ignore pressure as a mediating mechanism, and cannot produce interpretable causal estimates that distinguish the direct injection pathway from the pressure-mediated pathway.

Project Geminae addresses this gap by building an end-to-end causal pipeline on Texas Railroad Commission (RRC) injection records linked to USGS seismic event data, spanning 2010–2023 across 20 spatial radii. The pipeline implements seven successive modeling changes validated against physical domain expertise.

METHODS AND MATERIALS

Data: Texas RRC saltwater disposal records (650,374 well-month observations) linked spatially to USGS seismic catalog events (M ≥ 2.0) across radii of 1–20 km. Fault geometry derived from published Permian Basin fault shapefiles. Well-event linkage performed using a 90-day injection lookback window, selected empirically as optimal across a 7–90 day sensitivity sweep.

Causal Framework: A DAG with five nodes — injection volume W, wellhead pressure P, nearest fault distance G1, fault segment count G2, and earthquake magnitude S — is estimated using DoWhy’s backdoor adjustment. Four estimators run in parallel: OLS (baseline), GBM NonParamDML, Random Forest NonParamDML, and Lasso NonParamDML. Log-transforms are applied to W and P upstream. Missing pressure values are zero-filled with a binary P_missing indicator added as a covariate.

Hurdle Model: A two-stage framework separates earthquake occurrence from magnitude. Stage 1 fits a GBM binary classifier predicting P(S > 0). Stage 2 fits a GBM regressor predicting magnitude conditional on occurrence, trained only on event rows. Bootstrap confidence intervals (50 iterations) and DoWhy refutation tests (placebo, data subset) are run for all causal estimates.

RESULTS

The total causal effect of injection volume on earthquake magnitude is positive and significant at all 20 radii (p < 0.001), ranging from +7.95×10−² at 1 km to +1.00×10−¹ at 20 km in log-unit terms. The GBM NonParamDML estimator achieves an average predictive R² of 0.537 versus 0.411 for OLS, with a mean Spearman ρ of 0.672 and mean MAE of 0.453 magnitude units — a substantial improvement over the linear baseline.

The hurdle model Stage 1 classifier achieves ROC-AUC of 0.993 at 1 km, declining gradually to 0.971 at 20 km, demonstrating near-perfect discrimination between event and non-event rows using injection and fault geometry features alone. Occurrence probability declines from 76.2% at 1 km to 64.6% at 20 km — a 15 percentage-point attenuation consistent with pressure diffusion. Conditional magnitude E[M | occur] is stable across distance (range 1.634–1.747), indicating that once an event is triggered, expected magnitude is largely independent of distance from the injection well.

The pressure-mediated pathway (W→P→S) contributes a small negative indirect effect across all radii, with the proportion mediated ranging from −9.8% near-field to −3.0% far-field. This suggests pressure acts as a partial suppressor at these spatial scales rather than an amplifying mediator, and that the dominant injection-to-seismicity pathway is direct rather than pressure-routed.

DISCUSSION

The high Stage 1 AUC (>0.97 across all radii) reflects a strong spatial signal: fault proximity geometry (G1, G2) and injection volume are highly informative for discriminating event from non-event well-time periods. However, domain expert feedback indicates that formation depth is a critical unmodeled confounder. Shallow disposal zones (Grayburg, San Andres, Clearfork) are physically disconnected from seismogenic faults; only deep disposal zones (Ellenburger and below) are connected. The current model cannot differentiate by disposal interval without external well-completion records.

The stability of conditional magnitude across distance (Stage 2 range: 0.11 magnitude units over 20 km) was unexpected and has practical implications. It suggests that proximity to the wellbore does not appreciably change how large an event becomes once triggered — the magnitude distribution is set by the stress state of the fault, not by proximity to injection. This is consistent with the geomechanical view that fault rupture area, not pore pressure magnitude, controls event size.

Wellhead pressure as a proxy for subsurface pore pressure introduces known noise. Older wells with unmaintained wellbore-reservoir interfaces exhibit inflated wellhead pressure unrelated to subsurface conditions. The P_missing indicator (Change 6) addresses systematic gaps but does not correct noise in observed pressure values. Bottom-hole gauge data, where available, would materially improve the mediator measurement.

CONCLUSIONS

Project Geminae delivers the first fully causal, multi-radius pipeline for quantifying injection-to-seismicity effects in the Permian Basin. The pipeline is reproducible, configurable, and runs end-to-end from raw RRC data to dashboard visualization. Across 344,000 well-event linkages, injection volume has a consistent, statistically robust positive effect on local earthquake magnitude at all tested spatial scales.

The two-stage hurdle model demonstrates that injection predicts earthquake occurrence with near-perfect discriminability (AUC > 0.97), while conditional magnitude given occurrence is stable across distance (~M 1.68 mean). This decomposition is more actionable for operators than a single regression: Stage 1 informs site selection and risk screening, while Stage 2 informs consequence modeling for events that do occur.

Key future directions are formation-depth stratification (requiring disposal-interval records from IHS/Enverus or OGSL), extension of the lookback sweep beyond 90 days, and incorporation of fault orientation relative to regional stress field as an additional confounder.
