1. Abstract

Wastewater injection from oil and gas operations is the dominant driver of induced seismicity in the Permian Basin, yet the causal mechanisms — and the spatial scale over which they operate — remain incompletely characterized. We apply a structural causal model (DoWhy, NonParamDML) to 663,917 injection records from the Texas Railroad Commission (RRC) and 6,264 seismic events from the TexNet catalog to estimate the causal effect of injection volume on local magnitude across 20 spatial radii (1–20 km) and five lookback windows (7–90 days). Using a directed acyclic graph (DAG) that encodes injection volume (W) as the treatment, injection pressure (P) as a mediator, earthquake magnitude (S) as the outcome, and nearest fault distance and fault segment count as measured confounders, we decompose the total causal effect into direct (W → S) and pressure-mediated (W → P → S) pathways. Total effects are positive and significant at all 20 radii (p < 0.001); GBM nonparametric double machine learning achieves R² = 0.52–0.58, a 2.9× improvement over OLS. A 90-day lookback produces the best empirical fit and is adopted as the canonical window. A two-stage hurdle model further decomposes outcomes: Stage 1 (occurrence classifier, AUC 0.971–0.993) separates injection-triggered events from non-events; Stage 2 (conditional magnitude regressor, MAE ≈ 0.44) characterizes size given triggering. Occurrence probability declines 15 percentage points from 1 to 20 km while conditional magnitude remains stable (1.63–1.75), confirming that injection geometry governs whether an event occurs but not how large it becomes. Results support spatially targeted regulation within 7 km of known faults. Primary remaining limitation is the absence of disposal formation depth, which is required to distinguish seismogenic Ellenburger injectors from shallow non-seismogenic wells.

Keywords: induced seismicity · wastewater injection · causal inference · mediation analysis · Permian Basin · TexNet · directed acyclic graph · hurdle model

2. Introduction

Suggested length: ~600–800 words. Three subsections.

2.1   Background and Motivation

The central United States has experienced a dramatic rise in seismicity since approximately 2009, coinciding with the large-scale expansion of saltwater disposal (SWD) operations associated with unconventional oil and gas production. The Permian Basin in West Texas is among the most active regions, with the TexNet seismographic network documenting thousands of earthquakes since its deployment in 2017. Regulatory agencies and operators require evidence-based guidance on which wells pose the greatest seismic risk and at what spatial scales injection activity remains influential.

Prior work has established statistical associations between injection volume, pressure, and seismicity [cite Ellsworth 2013; Weingarten et al. 2015; Frohlich et al. 2016; Skoumal et al. 2020], but these studies are predominantly correlational. Correlation is insufficient for regulatory decisions: it does not distinguish causal pathways, cannot estimate counterfactual effects (what happens if injection is reduced by X%), and conflates direct mechanical effects with pressure-diffusion-mediated effects that operate over different spatial and temporal scales.

2.2   Gap in the Literature

Three specific gaps motivate this work:

  1. Causal identification. No published study has applied structural causal modeling (DAG + backdoor adjustment) to Permian Basin injection-seismicity data. Existing regression-based estimates do not control for the confounding structure between geology, injection practices, and seismicity.
  2. Mediation decomposition. Pressure is widely acknowledged as the physical intermediary between injection and fault reactivation, yet prior empirical work has not quantified what fraction of the total injection effect operates through pressure versus through direct mechanical or stress-loading pathways.
  3. Outcome model misspecification. Treating magnitude as a single continuous outcome conflates two physically distinct processes: the probability that an event is triggered at all, and the magnitude conditional on triggering. These may be governed by different physical and geological factors.

2.3   Contributions

This paper makes four contributions:

  1. A reproducible causal pipeline linking 663,917 RRC injection records to 6,264 TexNet events across 20 spatial radii, with bootstrap-validated effect estimates at each radius.
  2. A DAG-based mediation analysis decomposing the causal effect of injection volume into direct and pressure-mediated pathways, with nonparametric (GBM) double machine learning estimators that avoid linearity assumptions.
  3. An empirical lookback window sensitivity sweep (7–90 days) characterizing how temporal aggregation of injection volume affects the estimated causal relationship.
  4. A two-stage hurdle model that separates seismic occurrence from conditional magnitude, enabling independent characterization of injection’s role in triggering versus sizing earthquakes.

3.1   Filtering and Preprocessing

SWD records are filtered to the Permian Basin geographic bounding box and to the TexNet operational period. Records with missing or invalid well coordinates are retained but flagged. Seismic events are filtered to ML ≥ 1.0 to avoid catalog incompleteness at low magnitudes [cite Frohlich et al.]. Injection pressure is zero-filled (missing values set to 0, interpreted as “no reported pressure”) and a binary missingness indicator P_missing is added to the DAG as a measured covariate, separating structural zeros from reporting gaps (Change 6).

3.2   Spatial Join

Each seismic event is linked to all active injection wells within radius R (varied 1–20 km) using a planar coordinate system (EPSG:3857, Web Mercator). A well is considered “active” if it reported injection on the event date. “Innocent wells” — those spatially linked to an event but not injecting on the event date — are included as control rows with magnitude set to zero, preserving counterfactual structure in the dataset.

4.2   Identification Assumptions

Causal identification relies on three assumptions:

  1. Backdoor criterion. Conditioning on {G1, G2, Pmissing} blocks all non-causal paths between W and S. This requires that no unmeasured confounder simultaneously affects injection volume and seismicity after conditioning on fault proximity. Formation depth is a known candidate violation (Section 8).
  2. Positivity. Every combination of covariate values {G1, G2} appears for both high- and low-injection wells. Satisfied by the spatial diversity of the dataset.
  3. No interference. The seismic outcome for one well-event pair does not depend on injection at other wells beyond what is captured in the aggregated treatment variable.

5. Methods

Suggested length: ~700 words total across subsections.

5.1   Spatial Join and Radius Sensitivity (1–20 km)

Rather than selecting a single linkage radius, we run the full analysis at 20 radii from 1 to 20 km in 1 km increments. This produces 20 independent datasets (1,177 rows at 1 km to 344,161 rows at 20 km) and allows the spatial sensitivity of the causal estimate to be reported directly, rather than treated as a modeling assumption. The radius sweep also tests whether near-field and far-field injection-seismicity relationships differ mechanistically.

5.2   Injection Lookback Window Selection

The treatment variable W is the cumulative injection volume over the N days preceding each event. We perform a systematic sensitivity sweep across five windows (7, 14, 30, 60, 90 days) with all other pipeline parameters held constant. Window selection is performed in raw (non-log-transformed) space, as log-transformation introduces invariance to constant scaling (a multiplicative shift in log-space shifts the intercept but not the causal slope). The 90-day window is adopted as canonical based on monotonically improving predictive performance across all metrics (R², MAE, Spearman ρ), with the caveat that the improvement trend had not plateaued at 90 days.

5.3   Fault Proximity Features

Two geological control variables are derived from the Horne et al. (2023) fault shapefile. G1 is the Euclidean distance (km) from each well to the nearest fault segment, computed in EPSG:3857. G2 is the count of ~1 km fault segments within radius R of the well. Both variables are median-imputed where missing. These features serve as proxies for subsurface stress conditions that jointly influence injection practice (operators avoid faults) and seismic potential.

5.4   Missing Pressure Handling

Injection pressure P is missing for a non-trivial fraction of RRC records, reflecting a mix of idle wells (true zero pressure), optional reporting fields, and data gaps. Median imputation of missing P was used in initial runs but is known to bias the W → P and P → S path coefficients by assigning “typical” operating pressures to non-injecting rows. The production pipeline instead applies a two-part encoding: (1) zero-fill P (missing → 0, encoding “no reported pressure”), and (2) a binary indicator P_missing added to the DAG with edges from G1, G2, W, and to S. This separates the structural and informational content of missingness without introducing median-scale confounding.

5.5   Causal Estimators

We report four estimators per radius: OLS (linear baseline), GBM NonParamDML (primary), Random Forest NonParamDML, and Lasso. Nonparametric double machine learning (DML) estimates the causal effect via a two-stage residual-on-residual regression, with first-stage GBM models for E[W | confounders] and E[S | confounders]. This eliminates the linearity assumption of OLS while retaining the asymptotic properties of the DML estimator [cite Chernozhukov et al. 2018]. All models use 5-fold cross-fitting. Mediation decomposition follows the product-of-coefficients method for the indirect path W → P → S. Bootstrap confidence intervals (n = 200) are reported for all effect estimates.

5.6   Two-Stage Hurdle Decomposition

A hurdle model is fit separately from the causal estimator at each radius to decompose the outcome distribution. Stage 1 is a GBM binary classifier predicting whether an earthquake occurs (S > 0), trained on {W, P, G1, G2, Pmissing}. Stage 2 is a GBM regressor predicting ML conditional on occurrence, trained only on rows with S > 0. The hurdle outputs (occurrence probability, Stage 1 AUC, conditional magnitude, Stage 2 MAE) are reported alongside the causal estimates as complementary characterizations of the same outcome distribution.

6.3   Mediation Decomposition

At the 90-day canonical window, the pressure-mediated indirect path (W → P → S) accounts for −3% to −10% of the total effect across all radii (Table 3). The negative sign indicates that the pressure pathway opposes the total causal effect at this timescale. This reversal from the positive mediation observed at short windows (48% at 7 days, Section 6.2) suggests that at 90-day aggregation, cumulative pressure may partially dissipate stress or reflect operational pressure management, while the direct pathway captures cumulative mechanical loading.

Two findings stand out. First, Stage 1 AUC exceeds 0.97 at all radii, demonstrating near-perfect discrimination between event and non-event rows using only injection volume, pressure, and fault geometry. Second, conditional magnitude E[ML | occur] varies by only 0.11 magnitude units across all 20 radii, while occurrence probability P(occur) declines by 15 percentage points from 1 to 20 km. This asymmetry is the central interpretive finding of the hurdle decomposition: injection geometry determines whether events are triggered, not how large they become.

6.5   Predictive Validation

GBM NonParamDML achieves a mean R² of 0.537 and Spearman ρ of 0.672 across all radii at 90-day lookback, compared to OLS baselines of 0.411 and 0.609 respectively. Skill scores (1 − MSE/Var[S]) parallel R² throughout. These improvements are attributable to the nonlinear first-stage models in the DML residualization step, which better capture the non-monotone relationship between fault proximity and magnitude.

7. Discussion

Suggested length: ~500 words.

7.1   Physical Interpretation of the Two Pathways

The reversal of the pressure mediation sign between short (7-day) and long (90-day) lookback windows has a plausible physical explanation. At short windows, injection pressure spikes are the dominant signal: a sudden pressure increase directly stresses fault planes, and the pressure variable captures this acute effect as a positive mediator. At 90-day cumulative scales, injected volume captures total pore-pressure loading across the formation, while the pressure variable may reflect operating conditions that are partially managed or throttled in response to injection volume, introducing a negative feedback. This interpretation is speculative and requires domain confirmation from asset geologists familiar with Permian operational practice.

7.2   The Hurdle Decomposition and its Implications

The stable conditional magnitude across radii (1.63–1.75 ML) suggests that once a fault is critically stressed enough to rupture, the rupture magnitude is governed by fault geometry (segment length, orientation, normal stress) rather than by the injection conditions at the linked well. This is consistent with seismological theory: magnitude scales with fault area, which is a geological property, not an operational one. The implication is that seismic hazard mitigation should focus primarily on preventing triggering (Stage 1) rather than on limiting the size of triggered events.

7.3   Spatial Targeting and the 7 km Zone

P(occur) declines from 0.762 at 1 km to 0.646 at 20 km, a 15 percentage-point drop. The sharpest gradient occurs within 7 km, where P(occur) remains above 0.73. Combined with the total causal effect magnitude (which is also highest within 5 km), these results support spatially targeted regulation focusing on wells within 7 km of known active faults. Beyond 10 km, both the causal effect magnitude and occurrence probability stabilize, suggesting diminishing regulatory return per-well monitored at greater distances.

7.4   Comparison with Prior Work

Our causal effect estimates (∼0.08–0.10 log-magnitude per log-BBL) are not directly comparable to prior correlational coefficient estimates, as those studies do not apply backdoor adjustment or DML residualization. However, the direction, spatial attenuation profile, and pressure-mediation structure are broadly consistent with the physical conceptual model described in [cite Ellsworth 2013; Keranen & Weingarten 2018]. The contribution here is quantifying the causal magnitude and mediation structure with a reproducible, validated pipeline.

8. Limitations

Primary limitation: Disposal formation depth. Shallow injection zones (Grayburg, San Andres, Glorieta, Clearfork) are physically disconnected from the deep-seated faults attributed to seismicity in the Permian Basin. Only deep injection zones (Devonian and below, principally Ellenburger) are hydraulically connected to seismogenic fault systems. The current dataset does not differentiate by disposal formation; this information requires external well-completion records (IHS/Enverus or Texas OGSL) not available in the RRC injection data. The Stage 1 classifier partially learns this distinction through correlation with fault proximity (G1, G2), but cannot cleanly separate shallow from deep disposal wells. The high Stage 1 AUC likely reflects strong spatial correlation signal rather than complete formation-depth discrimination. Formation stratification is the highest-priority extension identified by the asset geology team (C. Reudelhueber, April 2026).

9. Conclusion and Policy Implications

We present the first causal mediation analysis of wastewater injection-induced seismicity in the Permian Basin, applied at scale across 20 spatial radii and five temporal lookback windows. The central findings are:

  1. Injection volume causally increases seismic magnitude at all tested radii (p < 0.001), with a consistent total effect of ∼0.08–0.10 log-magnitude units per log-BBL unit of injected volume (90-day window, GBM NonParamDML).
  2. The pressure pathway reverses sign at 90-day aggregation. Pressure mediates ∼48% of the total effect at 7-day windows but opposes the total effect (−3% to −10%) at 90 days, suggesting the dominant mechanism shifts from acute pressure spiking to cumulative mechanical loading at longer timescales.
  3. Injection geometry governs triggering, not magnitude. The two-stage hurdle model separates occurrence probability (0.646–0.762, strongly distance-dependent) from conditional magnitude (1.63–1.75, essentially distance-independent). This asymmetry has direct regulatory implications: monitoring and permitting decisions should prioritize wells within 7 km of known active fault systems.
  4. Nonparametric DML substantially outperforms OLS (R² 0.54 vs. 0.18 at 30-day lookback), confirming that the injection-seismicity relationship is nonlinear and that linear models understate predictive accuracy.

Policy recommendation: Spatially targeted regulation should focus on wells within 7 km of mapped active faults in the Permian Basin. Within that zone, cumulative 90-day injection volume is the most predictive trigger indicator currently available without formation depth data. Operators injecting into Ellenburger-depth formations within this zone represent the highest-priority monitoring tier.
