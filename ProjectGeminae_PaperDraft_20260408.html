Causal Risk Analytics for Injection-Induced Seismicity
in the Permian Basin: Mediation Analysis and Two-Stage
Hurdle Decomposition Across 20 Spatial Radii

Vivek Sriram  ·  Amitesh Mohan  ·  Raymond Tuo  ·  Janet Liu

Tepper School of Business, Carnegie Mellon University  ·  April 2026

MS Business Analytics Capstone  ·  Project Geminae  ·  vsriram@andrew.cmu.edu  ·  amiteshm@andrew.cmu.edu  ·  rtuo@tepper.cmu.edu  ·  janetliu@andrew.cmu.edu

Abstract.  Saltwater disposal (SWD) injection from oil and gas operations is the primary driver of induced seismicity in the Permian Basin of West Texas, creating operational, regulatory, and reputational risk for operators. Existing tools for assessing that risk are largely correlational and difficult to act on. This project builds an end-to-end causal analytics pipeline that moves beyond correlation to estimate the causal effect of injection activity on earthquake likelihood and magnitude. Using Texas Railroad Commission (RRC) injection records linked to USGS seismic events across 344,000 well-event observations, we apply a directed acyclic graph (DAG) and nonparametric double machine learning (GBM NonParamDML, DoWhy) to quantify how injection volume drives seismic outcomes while controlling for fault proximity, pressure, and geology. The pipeline delivers two actionable outputs: a causal effect estimate by distance from the wellbore (1–20 km), significant at p < 0.001 at all radii; and a two-stage hurdle model that separately predicts whether an earthquake will occur (Stage 1, AUC 0.97–0.99) and how large it will be if it does (Stage 2, MAE 0.44 ML). The GBM pipeline explains 54% of magnitude variance versus 41% for the linear baseline. Results are reproducible, configurable, and visualized in a live dashboard.

Keywords: induced seismicity · saltwater disposal · causal inference · directed acyclic graph · double machine learning · mediation analysis · hurdle model · Permian Basin

1. Introduction

The Permian Basin of West Texas accounts for a disproportionate share of U.S. induced seismicity. Operators injecting produced water — a byproduct of oil and gas extraction — into saltwater disposal (SWD) wells alter subsurface pore pressure, which can reactivate pre-existing faults and produce earthquakes. Regulatory bodies and operators urgently need quantitative tools to estimate injection-to-seismicity risk at the well level, yet the tools currently available are largely correlational: they identify statistical associations, but cannot tell an operator which wells are driving risk, by how much, or over what spatial range.

Correlation alone is insufficient for operational decision-making. Both injection volumes and earthquake rates are influenced by the same underlying geology — operators tend to locate high-volume disposal in favorable reservoir rock that may or may not coincide with seismogenic faults. Without accounting for this confounding structure, correlation-based estimates will misattribute geological risk to injection activity, or vice versa. Moreover, existing approaches conflate two physically distinct questions: (a) does injection trigger an earthquake at all, and (b) how large does that earthquake become once triggered? These are governed by different mechanisms — pore pressure diffusion and fault criticality control triggering; fault geometry and stress drop control magnitude — and deserve separate models [1].

This paper presents Project Geminae, an end-to-end causal pipeline built in direct partnership with a Permian Basin asset geologist [2] through seven iterative modeling changes. Our main contributions are:

  1. The first causal mediation analysis of SWD injection-induced seismicity at the well level in the Permian Basin, using nonparametric double machine learning estimators that avoid the linearity assumption of OLS [3].
  2. An empirical injection lookback window sweep (7–90 days) demonstrating that 90-day cumulative volumes produce substantially better predictive and causal models than shorter windows.
  3. A two-stage hurdle model that separates earthquake occurrence from conditional magnitude, yielding a physically interpretable and operationally actionable decomposition of seismic risk.

2. Data and Study Area

2.1 Study Area

The study covers the Permian Basin of West Texas, a sedimentary basin that is simultaneously one of the most productive oil fields in the world and one of the most seismically active induced-seismicity regions in the United States. The basin is underlain by a network of pre-existing faults, some of which are critically stressed. A key distinction — identified through domain expert consultation [2] — is that only deep-zone injectors (principally the Ellenburger formation) are hydraulically connected to seismogenic fault systems. Shallower disposal zones (Grayburg, San Andres, Clearfork) are physically disconnected from the deep fault network and are generally non-seismogenic. The current dataset does not differentiate by formation depth, which is the primary recommended extension (Section 6).

2.2 Injection Records (Texas RRC)

Injection data come from the Texas Railroad Commission (RRC) Saltwater Disposal database [6], which contains monthly reports filed by all permitted SWD operators in Texas. After filtering to the Permian Basin bounding area and the study period (2010–2023), we retain 650,374 well-month records. Each record includes injection volume (barrels, BBL), average and maximum wellhead pressure (PSIG), well surface coordinates, and report date. Wellhead pressure serves as a proxy for subsurface pore pressure, though it introduces known noise: older wells with degraded surface instrumentation can exhibit inflated wellhead readings unrelated to true subsurface conditions [2].

2.3 Seismic Catalog (USGS)

Seismic events come from the USGS Earthquake Catalog [7], filtered to the Permian Basin bounding area and to events ML ≥ 2.0. This yields 6,264 events with local magnitude, epicenter coordinates, and origin time. Events below M 2.0 are excluded due to catalog incompleteness at lower magnitudes in the region.

2.4 Fault Geometry

Fault locations and geometries come from a published Permian Basin fault shapefile, with segments approximately 1 km in length. We derive two geological control variables: G1 (distance in km from each injection well to the nearest fault segment) and G2 (count of fault segments within the spatial radius R of each well). These are median-imputed where missing and serve as proxies for local geological stress conditions.

2.5 Dataset Construction

For each USGS event, we identify all SWD wells within radius R (varied 1–20 km) that were actively injecting on the event date. Each linked well-event pair is enriched with the cumulative injection volume and average pressure from the 90 days preceding the event. Wells within R that were not injecting at the time are included as "non-event" control rows with magnitude set to zero. This produces datasets ranging from 1,177 rows at 1 km to 344,161 rows at 20 km.

3. Methods

3.1 Causal Framework: The Directed Acyclic Graph

A directed acyclic graph (DAG) is a formal tool for writing down assumptions about cause and effect. Each node is a variable; each arrow (\(A \rightarrow B\)) is a claim that \(A\) causally influences \(B\). The "acyclic" constraint means there are no feedback loops. DAGs are useful because they make confounding structure explicit, which allows us to apply the backdoor criterion — a rule for identifying which variables must be controlled to recover an unbiased causal estimate [4].

Our DAG has five nodes (Figure 1). The treatment \(W\) is 90-day cumulative injection volume; the mediator \(P\) is average wellhead pressure over the same window; the outcome \(S\) is local magnitude ML; and the confounders \(G_1\) and \(G_2\) are fault distance and fault segment count respectively. A confounder is a variable that independently drives both the treatment and the outcome. Here, geology (captured by \(G_1\), \(G_2\)) affects where operators choose to inject and the inherent seismic potential of the site. Without adjusting for geology, the observed injection-seismicity correlation will misrepresent the true causal effect.

The complete set of edges is: \(G_1 \rightarrow W,\, G_1 \rightarrow P,\, G_1 \rightarrow S,\; G_2 \rightarrow W,\, G_2 \rightarrow P,\, G_2 \rightarrow S,\; W \rightarrow P,\, W \rightarrow S,\, P \rightarrow S\). A sixth variable \(P_{\text{miss}}\) (binary indicator for missing wellhead pressure) is added with edges from \(G_1, G_2, W\) to \(S\), separating structural zeros from reporting gaps.

Figure 1. Causal DAG — Project Geminae. Nodes represent variables in the structural causal model; arrows represent direct causal relationships. G1 and G2 (fault geometry confounders) influence all three main variables. The W → P → S path is the pressure-mediated indirect effect; W → S is the direct effect.

The structural equation for the outcome and mediator are:

$$S_i = f\!\left(W_i,\, P_i,\, G_{1,i},\, G_{2,i},\, P_{\text{miss},i},\, \varepsilon_i\right), \qquad P_i = g\!\left(W_i,\, G_{1,i},\, G_{2,i},\, P_{\text{miss},i},\, \nu_i\right)$$

The backdoor criterion is satisfied by adjusting for \(\{G_1, G_2, P_{\text{miss}}\}\), which blocks all non-causal paths between \(W\) and \(S\), provided no unmeasured confounder simultaneously affects both. Formation depth (the Ellenburger vs. shallow-zone distinction) is a candidate unobserved confounder discussed in Section 6.

3.3 Treatment Variable and Lookback Window

The treatment \(W\) is the cumulative injection load in the \(N\) days before each event:

$$W_t^{(N)} = \sum_{\tau=1}^{N} w_{t-\tau}$$

where \(w_{t-\tau}\) is daily injection volume (BBL) at the linked well \(\tau\) days before event date \(t\). We sweep \(N \in \{7, 14, 30, 60, 90\}\) days; all predictive metrics improve monotonically up to 90 days (Table 2) and we adopt \(N = 90\) as the canonical window. Both \(W\) and \(P\) are log-transformed to linearize the volume-stress relationship and stabilize variance:

$$\tilde{W} = \log(1 + W^{(N)}), \qquad \tilde{P} = \log\!\left(1 + \max(P, 0)\right)$$

A key property of this transform: under a log-linear model, the causal slope is invariant to constant rescaling of \(W\). Window selection must therefore be performed on raw (untransformed) volumes, as we do in the sweep.

3.4 Missing Pressure Handling

Wellhead pressure \(P\) is missing for a non-trivial fraction of RRC records. Median imputation — the prior approach — assigns "typical operating pressure" to wells that may be genuinely idle, biasing the \(W \rightarrow P \rightarrow S\) mediation path. The production pipeline instead applies a zero-fill plus indicator encoding: missing \(P\) values are set to zero (encoding "no reported pressure"), and a binary variable \(P_{\text{miss}} = \mathbf{1}[P\text{ was missing}]\) is added to the DAG as a measured covariate. This transparently separates structural zeros from informative reporting gaps without distorting the pressure distribution.

3.5 Causal Estimation: Double Machine Learning

Double machine learning (DML) [3] estimates causal effects from observational data while allowing flexible, nonlinear nuisance functions. Starting from the partially linear model:

$$S_i = \theta \cdot W_i + g(\mathbf{X}_i) + \varepsilon_i, \qquad W_i = m(\mathbf{X}_i) + v_i$$

where \(\mathbf{X}_i = (G_{1,i}, G_{2,i}, P_{\text{miss},i})\), DML proceeds in two stages. First, flexible first-stage models (GBM) estimate and subtract the influence of confounders from both the treatment and outcome, yielding residuals:

$$\tilde{S}_i = S_i - \hat{g}(\mathbf{X}_i), \qquad \tilde{W}_i = W_i - \hat{m}(\mathbf{X}_i)$$

The residual \(\tilde{W}_i\) is the component of injection volume that cannot be explained by geology — the "surprise" injection that is exogenous with respect to confounders. The second stage recovers the causal effect by regressing \(\tilde{S}_i\) on \(\tilde{W}_i\):

$$\hat\theta = \left(\sum_i \tilde{W}_i^2\right)^{-1} \sum_i \tilde{W}_i \tilde{S}_i$$

We implement this via DoWhy's NonParamDML estimator [5] with 5-fold cross-fitting. The total causal effect decomposes as:

$$\underbrace{\theta}_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{\delta}_{\text{direct } (W \to S)} + \underbrace{\kappa}_{\text{indirect } (W \to P \to S)}$$

Four estimators run in parallel — OLS (linear baseline), GBM NonParamDML (primary), Random Forest NonParamDML, and Lasso NonParamDML — with 200-iteration bootstrap confidence intervals and DoWhy refutation tests (placebo treatment, data subset) for all estimates.

3.6 Two-Stage Hurdle Model

A hurdle model is designed for outcomes that are zero with high probability and positive otherwise — exactly the structure of our data, where most well-date pairs have no associated earthquake. Rather than a single regression that conflates triggering and magnitude, we fit two separate models:

Stage 1 — Occurrence. A GBM binary classifier predicts whether any earthquake occurs:

$$\Pr(S > 0 \mid \mathbf{x}) = \sigma\!\left(f_1(W, P, G_1, G_2, P_{\text{miss}})\right)$$

Evaluated on a held-out test set by ROC-AUC. Intuitively, Stage 1 answers: given this well's injection profile and fault proximity, is an earthquake expected to occur?

Stage 2 — Conditional magnitude. A separate GBM regressor predicts expected magnitude given that an earthquake did occur, trained only on event rows:

$$E[S \mid S > 0,\, \mathbf{x}] = f_2(W, P, G_1, G_2, P_{\text{miss}})$$

Evaluated by mean absolute error (MAE) in magnitude units. Stage 2 answers: given that an event is triggered, how large should we expect it to be?

4. Results

4.1 Lookback Window Selection

Table 2 shows predictive performance as a function of lookback window, averaged across all 20 radii (GBM NonParamDML, raw space without log-transform, to expose genuine variation across windows). Every metric improves monotonically from 7 to 90 days. The 90-day window achieves R² = 0.537, Spearman \(\rho\) = 0.672, and MAE = 0.454 ML. Notably, the pressure mediation proportion also decreases with window length: at 7 days, 48% of the total effect routes through the pressure pathway; at 90 days, only 17% does. This shift indicates that acute pore pressure spikes dominate at short timescales, while cumulative mechanical loading (the direct pathway) dominates at longer ones. The 90-day trend had not plateaued, suggesting the true optimal window may exceed 90 days for tight Permian formations.

4.2 Model Performance: OLS vs. GBM

Figure 3 compares predictive R² across all 20 radii for OLS and GBM NonParamDML, both at 90-day lookback. GBM outperforms OLS at every radius. The improvement peaks at 2 km (+22% relative), where the GBM captures near-field nonlinear injection effects that OLS cannot represent. On average, GBM explains 54% of magnitude variance versus 41% for OLS — a 31% relative improvement. This confirms that the injection-seismicity relationship is substantially nonlinear and that standard linear regression materially understates both predictive accuracy and, by extension, the precision of causal effect estimates.

4.3 Total Causal Effect Across Radii

The total causal effect of log-injection-volume on local magnitude is positive and statistically significant at all 20 radii (bootstrap p < 0.001 throughout). Table 3 shows selected results. The effect rises from +0.080 at 1 km to +0.094 at 3 km as more seismically relevant well-event pairs enter the dataset, then stabilizes in the range +0.094–0.100 for radii 3–20 km. In plain terms: a one log-unit increase in 90-day injection volume (roughly a 2.7× increase in volume) corresponds to an increase in local magnitude of approximately +0.095 ML. The pressure-mediated indirect path (W → P → S) accounts for a small negative proportion of the total effect at all radii (−9.8% near-field to −3.0% far-field), meaning the direct pathway carries more than the full total effect.

4.4 Two-Stage Hurdle Model

Figure 4 shows the hurdle model's two outputs across all 20 radii, revealing a striking asymmetry between the two stages of earthquake production.

Stage 1 — Does it occur? Occurrence probability P(S > 0) declines from 76% at 1 km to 65% at 20 km — a 15 percentage-point drop consistent with pressure diffusion attenuating with distance. Stage 1 ROC-AUC is remarkably high throughout: 0.993 at 1 km and 0.971 at 20 km, demonstrating near-perfect discrimination between event and non-event rows using only injection volume, wellhead pressure, and fault geometry. In practical terms, the model can almost perfectly identify which well-date pairs correspond to real seismic events.

Stage 2 — How large? Conditional magnitude E[ML | S > 0] ranges only from 1.747 at 3 km to 1.634 at 20 km — a span of 0.11 ML over a 20 km range. The mean is 1.68 ML and the curve is essentially flat across distance. Once an earthquake is triggered, proximity to the injection well does not appreciably change how large it becomes. Stage 2 MAE averages 0.44 ML across all radii, meaning the model's conditional magnitude predictions are within roughly half a magnitude unit on average.

Figure 4. Two-stage hurdle model | GBM NonParamDML | 90-day lookback | 1,177–344,161 well-event observations per radius. Left: Stage 1 occurrence probability P(S > 0) declines monotonically from 76% to 65% over 1–20 km. Right: Stage 2 conditional magnitude E[ML | S > 0] is stable across distance (range 0.11 ML; mean 1.68). Proximity to injection controls whether an event happens, not how large it becomes.

This asymmetry is the central interpretive finding of the hurdle analysis. It implies that the two components of seismic hazard are governed by fundamentally different physical mechanisms: triggering is a fluid mechanics problem (pore pressure diffusion to a critically stressed fault plane), while magnitude is a structural geology problem (fault rupture area and stress drop, which are properties of the fault system, not of the injection well).

5. Discussion

5.1 Physical Interpretation of the Pressure Pathway

At short lookback windows (7 days), roughly 48% of the total injection effect routes through the pressure pathway (W → P → S), consistent with the physical intuition that acute pressure spikes are the primary short-term fault trigger. As the window extends to 90 days, this fraction shrinks to 17% and the indirect path sign reverses. One plausible explanation is that 90-day average wellhead pressure reflects an operationally managed quantity — operators throttle pressure when volumes are high to stay within permitted limits — rather than the true subsurface pore pressure at the fault. Under this reading, the pressure mediator at long windows captures an attenuated, managed signal, and the dominant injection-to-seismicity mechanism at 90-day aggregation is cumulative mechanical loading (the direct path), not acute pressure propagation. Domain expert consultation [2] flagged that wellhead pressure as a proxy for subsurface conditions introduces noise correlated with well age and maintenance history, which may compound this effect.

5.2 Regulatory Implications of the Hurdle Decomposition

The stability of conditional magnitude (0.11 ML span over 20 km) has a direct geomechanical interpretation: earthquake magnitude is primarily controlled by fault rupture area, a geological property, not by the injection conditions at the triggering well. Once a fault is critically stressed, the size of the resulting rupture is set by the fault's geometry, not by how close the injection well is. Seismic hazard management should therefore focus almost entirely on preventing triggering — Stage 1 — rather than attempting to limit earthquake size once triggered. The strong distance dependence of P(occur) (76% → 65% over 1–20 km) supports spatially targeted regulation focusing on wells within 7 km of mapped active fault structures as the highest-leverage intervention point.

The Stage 1 and Stage 2 decomposition is also operationally more actionable than a single regression output. Stage 1 informs site selection, permitting prioritization, and real-time monitoring thresholds. Stage 2 informs consequence modeling (structural risk assessment, emergency response planning) for events that do occur. A single regression coefficient conflates these two physically distinct management levers.

5.3 Radial Distance as a Proxy

The radial distance framework assumes isotropic pressure propagation — that injection pressure spreads equally in all directions. Domain expert feedback [2] identifies this as a known physical limitation: pressure actually propagates preferentially along fault planes and permeable formation pathways, not radially. A well 3 km from a fault along its strike may exert far more influence than a well 3 km away in a direction perpendicular to the fault. The current model captures fault proximity through G1 and G2, but does not encode fault orientation relative to the regional stress field. Incorporating fault azimuth and critically stressed fault plane geometry would materially improve both the causal estimates and the hurdle model's Stage 1 AUC.

6. Limitations and Future Work

Primary limitation: The dataset does not differentiate injection wells by disposal formation depth. Shallow disposal zones (Grayburg, San Andres, Clearfork) are physically disconnected from seismogenic faults; only deep zones (Ellenburger and below) drive seismicity in the Permian Basin [2]. Without disposal-interval data from IHS/Enverus or the Texas OGSL, the pipeline cannot make this separation. Formation depth stratification is the single highest-impact improvement available and the primary recommendation from our domain expert collaborator.

7. Conclusion

Project Geminae delivers the first end-to-end causal seismic risk pipeline built directly on RRC and USGS data for the Permian Basin. Across 344,000 well-event linkages at 90-day lookback, injection volume has a consistent, statistically robust positive causal effect on earthquake magnitude at all 20 tested spatial radii (p < 0.001). The nonlinear GBM pipeline explains 54% of magnitude variance versus 41% for the linear baseline, and the two-stage hurdle model gives operators two distinct, actionable outputs: the probability an event occurs (Stage 1, AUC 0.97–0.99) and its expected magnitude if it does (Stage 2, MAE 0.44 ML).

The central finding is a clean physical asymmetry: occurrence probability declines from 76% to 65% over 1–20 km (distance-dependent), while conditional magnitude is stable at approximately M 1.68 across the same range (distance-independent). Proximity to injection controls whether an event happens; fault structure controls how large it becomes. This decomposition is more actionable than a single regression coefficient and directly informs spatially targeted regulation within 7 km of mapped fault structures.

Formation depth stratification — filtering the pipeline to deep disposal zones (Ellenburger and below) once disposal-interval records become available — is the single highest-priority extension. Additional directions include extending the lookback sweep beyond 90 days, incorporating fault orientation relative to regional stress as a confounder, and validating the pipeline against bottom-hole pressure gauge data where available.
